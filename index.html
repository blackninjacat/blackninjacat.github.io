Allopatric Speciation.

Allopatric Speciation is due to geographical isolation. For example, by a mountain range or a large expanse of an ocean. The physical barrier isolates part of the original population and prevents gene flow between the two separated populations. Both sub-populations are now considered to be reproductively isolated from each other. When the environmental conditions on either side of the barrier vary, different selection pressures are imposed upon the two sub-populations. This causes different alleles to be selected for in both sub-populations. Mutations may also occur, introducing new alleles into one sub-population but they will not be present in the other sub-population due to the physical barrier preventing free gene flow. Allele frequencies will then change in both populations. Over a long period of time and over many generations, both sub-populations become so genetically different until they are no longer genetically identical. Both may now be considered as separate species as they are now no longer capable of interbreeding.

Role of Natural Selection in Evolution

Organisms produce more offspring than needed to replace its dead members. Individuals of the population show genetic and phenotypic variation. Due to environmental factors that provide selection pressures, those best adapted to the current conditions survive and reproduce as they are individuals who are selected for. As a result, many do not survive and so the population remains roughly constant. Over a long period of time and many generations, allele frequencies change and organisms evolve
